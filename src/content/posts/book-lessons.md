### Kitchen Confidential

1. You don't need to like somone to be able to enjoy spending time with them.
   Weird as it sounds, I realized this is true. You don't need to get caught up in your dislike to the point
   of not enjoying time with them. Someone who you don't like can still be interesting.
   At the very least, it'll be fun to think back on later. Obviosuly this doesn't mean
   to go out of your way to hang out with people you don't like, it just means
   that if you need to spend time with someone you ordinarily wouldn't want to,
   you might as well enjoy it.
2. You need to get used to criticism. Most people probably agree intellectually, but you
   need to get to the point where even being yelled at doesn't affect you. I'm still
   working on this, but I'd like to get there. There's really no reason to take it personally.

### The Fountainhead

1. There's a lot to deconstruct about Ayn Rand, and I certainly have my fair share
   of critiques of her philosophy. But the big thing I got out of the book is the reason
   why it's one of my favorites: I realized that Howard is genuinely happier than Peter.
   I deeply understood, for the first time in my life, how the popular, wealthy,
   successful guy who gets all the women and has his name on all the newspapers is
   not nearly as happy as the man who lives with integrity. It's a simple idea, but
   it changed my life. There's so much more to life than the usual things society tells
   us we should pursue, and as cliché as that seems, for a long time it was something
   that I knew intellectually but not on a deep, emotional level. Since reading this
   book, I no longer want to be famous.

### Goodbye, Things

1. Owning less stuff is actually nice. Being a minimalist isn't about
   making scarafices and pinching pennies in the name of some abstract ideal.
   It's about understanding that life is better when your things don't
   own you. Since reading this book, I've been a lot better about not owning
   possessions that don't add value to my life. I'm stil learning how to do
   this, but I've started asking "is this item really going to make me happier?" and
   understanding how the answer might actually be "No."

### The Elephant in the Brain

1. People, and society as a whole, are really bad at understanding why we do
   what we do. To me, this is a warrant to intensely question norms and
   willingly deviate from them. The reasons they exist are probably
   not what you think at first, and most people aren't honest with
   themselves about why they exist. The chapter on college education
   particularly opened my eyes. If you ask someone why they value a
   college education (either a student or an employey), the answer
   will probably involve education. Why then can you often audit a
   college class for free? Why does being one credit shy of graduation
   not ential 95% of the value of a college degree. At the least,
   there's something weird going on here that needs to be unpacked.
   I need to question things like this about society and not blindly accept
   these norms and valuations.

### Extreme Ownership

1. It's up to the leader of a team to make things work. If the team isn't
   effective, there's _always_ something the leader can do to imrove the
   situation.
2. Team chemistry and character matters more than raw skills. Select
   teammates who have strong values that align with the groups. Skills
   can come later. And worry at least as much about building your
   character as your skills.

### Non-violent Communication

1. Every action you take is in pursuit of some goal that you regard
   as being good. Use this fact to find compassion for what people do,
   including yourself.

### The Quest for Character

1. The character of a leader matters _a lot_. This is kind of an
   obvious conclusion but after reading the book I've played around
   with the idea of voting based on character instead of policy.
   So maybe this guy will do things I don't agree with but at least
   I trust that s/he's doing it for a reason that s/he genuinely believes in.
   Maybe that's a dumb idea, I'm still not sure. Would I rather have a
   demogogue who does everything I want or an honest person who
   does things I don't agree with?

### Talking to Strangers

1. If you think you're "good at reading people" then you're probably wrong.
   Don't trust your ability to judge a person's character after a meeting or two.
   It's okay to not completely have a person figured out. I feel like there's a lot
   of people who pride themselves on being a good judge of character, but it's
   important to keep your limits in mind.
